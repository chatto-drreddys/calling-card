# Max & Max: Leadership Parable Reflection

## Context
The Max & Max parable teaches about leadership expectations, management styles, and the gap between stated values and actual behavior. It uses a parallel story of Max (the dog) and Max (the man) under the leadership of Mr. Harold.

---

## Personal Reflection Questions

### 1. Have you ever experienced having a boss like Mr Harold?

Based on the parable, Mr. Harold exhibits:

- **Unclear expectations** — He hires Max but doesn't articulate what success looks like
- **Policy-bound thinking** — Hides behind "company policy" rather than coaching toward results
- **Reactive feedback** — Only responds when things go wrong ("Why did you do that?") rather than proactively guiding
- **Slow to recognize value** — Doesn't see Max's initiative until forced into a corner

Many workplaces have Harolds. The key signal: **the boss seems frustrated by your solutions even when they work.**

### 2. How would you handle work with a boss like Mr Harold?

Three strategies:

- **Decode what he *actually* wants** (not what he *says*) — Max (the dog) learned through repetition and observation; Max (the man) should have done the same earlier

- **Document the results** — When you solve the shipment problem, make sure Harold sees the outcome (sales, customer satisfaction), not just the method

- **Find the permission in the crisis** — Harold only empowers Max when customers complain and Harold's own status is threatened. Don't wait; escalate thoughtfully when policy blocks service

### 3. How would you describe Mr Harold as a boss?

**Well-intentioned but trapped.** Harold:

- Genuinely cares about company goals and service (mission on wall)
- But conflates "control through policy" with "leadership"
- Is risk-averse and uses company guidelines as a shield
- Learns slowly — needs Max to *show* him (through the kayak example) rather than *tell* him
- Responds to pressure rather than proactively creating psychological safety

**In short:** A **command-and-control manager** masquerading as mission-driven.

### 4. What would you do if you were Max and Max?

#### As Max (the dog):
- Learn Harold's patterns and preferences → adapt → succeed through feedback loops
- Take initiative *within observed boundaries* → let results speak

#### As Max (the man):
- Same strategy initially, but escalate differently:
  - Document shipment fix + result (sales/customer retention)
  - Propose a pilot: "Sir, what if we test the approach on this one customer and track whether it improves satisfaction?"
  - Involve the customer: Have the customer tell Harold directly why the solution mattered
  - Don't hide behind policy compliance; lead with service outcomes

## The 6 "Cancers" of Management Applied to Mr. Harold

These toxins erode trust and innovation:

| Cancer | How Harold Exhibits It | The Damage |
|--------|------------------------|-----------|
| **Cynicism** | "Why would you go into the water?" (assumes bad motives for initiative) | Max (man) stops proposing solutions; Max (dog) learns to freeze |
| **Criticism** | Constant questioning ("Why did you do that?") without context-setting | Blame culture replaces learning culture |
| **Comparing** | "Company policy says..." (measuring Max against rules, not against mission) | Goal-misalignment: Max serves policy, not customers |
| **Competing** | Harold protects his turf; won't empower Max to solve customer calls | Scarcity mindset; Harold's promotion > Team's success |
| **Complaining** | "Budget issues," "mind it" (victimhood language) | Team internalizes helplessness |
| **Contending** | The kayak incident: Harold falls in, Max (man) starts to help, Max (dog) stops him | Unclear whose job is what; no mutual trust |

**The pattern:** Harold uses these six cancers to *maintain control* while appearing to want *service and contribution.* Max (the man) internalized this disconnect and became disgusted. Max (the dog) simply learned Harold's pattern and adapted.

---

## What Could Have Been Done Differently by Mr. Harold

### Harold's needed shift:

**1. Clarity on the real mission**
   - Walk Max (both of them) through: "Here's what I actually value: solving customer problems, even if it breaks policy"
   - "Here's how you'll know you're winning: [specific metrics]"

**2. Psychological safety**
   - Respond to Max's shipment solution with curiosity, not suspicion
   - "Tell me how you solved this. What did you learn? How can we scale it?"

**3. Reverse the feedback loop**
   - Don't wait for Max to fail
   - *Ask* Max: "What are the barriers you hit this week? What would you try if policy weren't in the way?"

**4. Lead by example in the kayak moment**
   - When he falls in, *let* Max (man) help
   - Show vulnerability
   - Demonstrate that mission > ego

**5. Remove the cancers:**
   - Replace cynicism with curiosity
   - Replace criticism with coaching
   - Replace competing with collaboration
   - Replace policy with principles (e.g., "Service trumps process")

## Key Observations

### The parable's power:

**Max (the dog) succeeds because he has no internal dialogue**
- He observes, adapts, executes
- He doesn't defend himself or explain his motives

**Max (the man) struggles because he has questions Harold won't answer**
- "Why did you hire me?"
- "Why are you angry?"
- "Why do you care about policy more than service?"
- This gap makes him disgusted

**The kayak fall is the inflection point**
- Harold is *vulnerable*, and both Maxes must decide: do we help, or do we protect ourselves?
- The hat in the water is the test of whether they've internalized the mission or just survived the system

### For your leadership:

- **Avoid becoming Harold** by making your *actual* values and *actual* measurement criteria crystal clear
- **Watch for the six cancers** in yourself; they often hide in "just being realistic" or "protecting the team"
- **Remember:** Your team will learn *what you reward*, not *what you say*

## Connection to Habit 8

The 8th Habit is about **finding your voice and inspiring others to find theirs.** 

Harold *never found his voice* — he defaulted to company policy instead of his own conviction. He never *inspired* Max to find *his* voice — instead, he confused Max and made him disgusted.

A leader with the 8th Habit would:
- Model authentic leadership (what you truly believe, not what the handbook says)
- Create space for others to discover and express their gifts
- Challenge without cynicism
- Lead by example in vulnerability
- Measure success by contribution, not compliance
